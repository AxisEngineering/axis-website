CLAIM VERIFICATION SUMMARY

1. Claim: a LIM is effectively a rotary induction motor "unrolled"
Status: Supported.

The Federal Railroad Administration technical background report includes the
classic explanation that a linear induction machine can be introduced by
imagining a conventional rotary stator cut and unrolled. The site wording is a
plain-English version of accepted LIM theory.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


2. Claim: three-phase primary windings create a travelling magnetic field that
induces currents in a conductive reaction plate
Status: Supported.

The FRA report describes propulsion in a linear motor as a reaction between
currents induced in a conducting plate and a travelling magnetic field produced
by a distributed polyphase winding.

The site wording is accurate.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


3. Claim: field speed is set by drive frequency and pole spacing; slip is one
of the variables setting thrust
Status: Supported.

This is standard LIM/induction-machine theory. The site now avoids saying the
plate "always" runs slower, and limits the statement to normal motoring. That
is the right caveat because braking, plugging, rollback and control limits can
change the operating case.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep current cautious wording.


4. Claim: thrust comes from air-gap power and is refined by end-effect and
edge-effect corrections
Status: Supported in principle.

The literature supports use of equivalent-circuit methods, FEA cross-checks and
explicit treatment of longitudinal end effects and transverse edge effects.
The FRA report highlights end/edge effects as a key difference from rotary
machines, and later LIM papers use analytical and finite-element modelling to
account for them.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


5. Claim: LIM propulsion is adhesion-independent
Status: Supported, with correct boundary.

Bombardier's urban transit paper states that LIM propulsion transmits
acceleration and braking forces independently of wheel-rail adhesion, and
documents ART/SkyTrain/JFK style systems using this principle.

The site wording is now good because it says the propulsion/braking thrust is
not limited by wheel-rail grip. It does not claim the whole vehicle has no
wheel contact or no guidance/friction anywhere.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep. If extra precision is wanted, add "for propulsion" or
"for the drive force" when discussing adhesion independence.


6. Claim: people movers and transit systems can use LIM propulsion for wet,
icy or steep conditions
Status: Supported.

The Bombardier paper specifically discusses all-weather operation, snow/ice,
precision stopping, high-grade capability and existing ART systems using LIM
propulsion.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


7. Claim: SSLIM has significant normal attraction toward steel backing
Status: Supported.

The literature supports that single-sided LIMs produce normal forces in
addition to longitudinal thrust, and that these forces can be structurally
important. Bombardier's transit paper notes vertical force can be equal to or
higher than thrust in a single-sided topology.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


8. Claim: DSLIM can approach twice the active-face thrust under comparable
current, gap and thermal limits
Status: Supported as a bounded engineering statement.

A double-sided LIM uses two active faces working on the same conductive fin.
Research literature describes double-sided LIMs as attractive for high force
density and simple passive tracks. The site's "can approach" and "under
comparable limits" wording is important and should stay.

Risk level: Medium if the caveats are removed. Low with the current wording.
Recommendation: Keep "can approach" and "under comparable current, gap and
thermal limits." Do not shorten this back to "gives 2x thrust."


9. Claim: opposing normal pulls in a DSLIM largely cancel in the frame
Status: Supported with caveats.

The principle is sound for symmetric double-sided arrangements. Real machines
can still see residual normal/side forces from air-gap imbalance, tolerances,
saturation and alignment errors. The current wording says "largely cancel",
which is good.

Risk level: Low with current wording.
Recommendation: Keep "largely". Avoid "fully cancel", "zero normal force", or
"the vehicle never feels it."


10. Claim: permanent-magnet eddy-current brakes are contactless and need no
external power while moving
Status: Supported.

Permanent-magnet eddy-current brake suppliers describe these as power-free
deceleration systems requiring relative motion between magnet assembly and
conductive plate. Research papers also support the contactless eddy-current
mechanism.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


11. Claim: PM eddy-current brake force rises from standstill, reaches a broad
peak, and can taper at higher speeds
Status: Supported.

The MDPI high-speed maglev eddy-current braking paper describes peak braking
force and critical speed, and notes that braking force decreases once speed is
beyond the critical speed. It also analyses how air gap, pole pitch, plate
thickness and conductivity affect the curve.

The site wording is technically sound.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


12. Claim: PM eddy-current brakes do not hold at standstill
Status: Supported.

This is an important caveat. A permanent-magnet eddy-current brake needs
relative motion to generate eddy currents. H2W's product documentation states
that with no relative velocity, no braking occurs. The current site correctly
says a held standstill needs a paired mechanical holding brake.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep this caveat visible on every PM brake page.


13. Claim: launch systems and roller-coaster acceleration can use linear
motor technology
Status: Supported as a general application claim.

Linear motor launch systems are well established, especially LSM launch systems
in modern coasters. LIM launch systems also exist historically. The Axis page
is safe because it refers to launch duties generally rather than claiming Axis
has supplied a specific ride.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep, but do not imply Axis has delivered named ride systems
unless you can publish evidence.


14. Claim: maglev propulsion commonly uses linear motor thrust
Status: Supported.

FTA/Sandia material compares LIM and LSM propulsion options for urban and
suburban maglev systems and describes practical maglev/linear-metro use cases.
The site also correctly separates propulsion from levitation and guidance.

Risk level: Low.
Recommendation: Keep.


15. Claim: Axis uses equivalent-circuit models, FEA, route simulation and
thermal modelling
Status: Internally supported, not publicly proven.

Local Axis files support this claim. Evidence found in the Axis folder includes:
- OPT 33 VBA modules: Calc_L.bas, Calc_A.bas, Calc_B.bas, Track_Sim.bas,
  Thermal_Dissipation.bas, FEA_Launch.bas and related solver/output modules.
- GetDP/Elmer FEA coverage folder with scripts, CSV sweeps, validation reports
  and coverage-factor findings.
- Validation reports discussing FEA coverage factors, current limiting,
  thermal and brake solver verification.

This makes the site claim credible from the local evidence available to me.
However, a public visitor cannot independently see the proof.

Risk level: Medium for trust/conversion, not for physics.
Recommendation: Add at least one public "sample output" or "what you receive"
section: example thrust-speed curve, thermal duty plot, route simulation output,
or anonymised analysis report excerpt.


16. Claim: "2x independent solver methods"
Status: Plausible from local evidence, but not externally verifiable.

The local engineering folder contains analytical/VBA modelling and GetDP/Elmer
FEA workflows, so the claim appears fair. Public trust would be stronger if
the site showed a sample comparison plot or a short explanation of the two
solver routes.

Risk level: Medium for proof.
Recommendation: Add a small "model cross-check" graphic or sample report image.


17. Claim: "0 powertrain contact points"
Status: Mostly fair, but depends on interpretation.

For LIM thrust and eddy-current braking, the working force is developed across
an air gap, so the drive/brake interface has no frictional contact. However,
vehicles may still have wheels, guide rollers, bearings or mechanical support
interfaces.

The word "powertrain" keeps the claim defensible.

Risk level: Low to medium.
Recommendation: Keep "powertrain contact points"; do not shorten it to "zero
contact points" or "no contact wear" across the whole vehicle.


AI-SLOP / WORDING CHECK

The current site does not read like obvious AI slop. It is specific to LIMs,
DSLIMs, PM eddy-current brakes, air gaps, slip, reaction plates, FEA and
thermal duty. The copy uses engineering caveats and avoids many generic claims.

The strongest remaining marketing phrases are acceptable, but proof would help:
- "Every number traceable to a validated method"
- "2x independent solver methods"
- "100% of the route simulated"
- "We build what we design"

These are not technically wrong, but they would be stronger with screenshots,
sample plots, a short case-study style note, or an anonymised deliverable.
